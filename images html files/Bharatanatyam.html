Bharatanatyam

Bharatanatyam
Bharatanatyam is a classical Indian dance form that originated in the temples of Tamil Nadu, India, and has a history spanning over 2,000 years. It combines intricate footwork, expressive hand gestures (mudras), facial expressions, and storytelling to convey mythological tales, historical narratives, and emotions.

Dancers wear traditional attire, including colorful sarees and jewelry, and adorn their hair with flowers. The dance is accompanied by classical Carnatic music, featuring instruments like the mridangam, veena, and flute.

Bharatanatyam is known for its precision and graceful movements, blending nritta (pure dance) with nritya (expressive dance) and natya (drama). It has a rich repertoire of mudras and is characterized by its devotion to Hindu deities, particularly Lord Shiva and Lord Krishna.

The dance form has evolved over time and continues to adapt to contemporary themes while preserving its traditional essence. It is a testament to India's rich cultural heritage and remains a revered and captivating art form enjoyed by audiences worldwide.


Origin and History
Bharatanatyam, one of India's oldest classical dance forms, traces its origins to the temples of Tamil Nadu in South India. The name "Bharatanatyam" is derived from "Bha" (Bhava - expression), "Ra" (Raga - melody), "Ta" (Tala - rhythm), and "Natyam" (dance). This dance form has a history that spans over 2,000 years, with its roots in the Natya Shastra, an ancient treatise on performing arts. Over the centuries, Bharatanatyam has evolved, transitioning from temple rituals to a highly stylized and revered classical dance form.
Significance
Bharatanatyam is not just a dance but a cultural and spiritual experience. It is deeply intertwined with Hindu mythology, religious rituals, and the expression of devotion. This dance form serves as a medium to narrate stories from epics like the Ramayana and Mahabharata, communicate religious tales, and convey emotions, making it an integral part of India's cultural heritage.
Unique Features
Bharatanatyam is renowned for its striking combination of precise footwork, graceful movements, and intricate expressions. It seamlessly combines nritta (pure dance), nritya (expressive dance), and natya (dramatic storytelling). The dance's unique ability to blend artistic beauty with profound storytelling sets it apart.



Costume
Dancers in Bharatanatyam wear traditional attire, typically a vibrant silk saree with an intricately designed border. The costume also includes an intricately designed blouse. The hair is tied up in a traditional bun adorned with fragrant flowers, and jewelry like temple jewelry, anklets, and bangles complete the ensemble. The attire reflects a sense of cultural richness and elegance.
Music
Bharatanatyam is accompanied by classical Carnatic music, which includes vocal music, rhythmic percussion from instruments like the mridangam, melodic accompaniment from instruments like the violin, flute, and veena, and occasionally the use of a tambura for drone. The music is an integral part of the dance, with compositions in various ragas and talas that synchronize with the dancer's movements, enhancing the overall aesthetics of the performance. The melodic and rhythmic patterns of Carnatic music provide a rich and dynamic backdrop to the dance, elevating its artistic beauty.



Asamyukta Hasthas or the Single-Hand Gestures
Pataka:
The Pataka hand gesture in Bharatanatyam involves extending the thumb and the little finger while folding the other fingers. This mudra symbolizes a flag or banner. It is often used in dance to represent victory, royalty, or the presence of a king. The Pataka mudra adds a regal and triumphant element to the dancer's expressions, making it a significant gesture in the repertoire of Bharatanatyam. Its distinct shape and meaning contribute to the storytelling and emotive aspects of the dance.


Tripataka:
Tripataka is a hand gesture in Bharatanatyam, representing a flag or banner with three distinct parts. To form Tripataka, extend the thumb, the little finger, and the ring finger while keeping the other fingers folded. This mudra is employed to depict various objects or concepts related to triumphant narratives, divisions, or multiplicity. It adds depth and symbolism to the dancer's expressions, enhancing the storytelling aspect of the dance, and it's a part of the rich vocabulary of hand gestures in this classical Indian dance form.


Ardhapataakam:
The "Ardha Pataka" hand gesture in Bharatanatyam is formed by extending the little finger while folding the other fingers. This elegant mudra symbolizes a half flag or banner. It represents a sense of division or sharing in the narrative, conveying subtle emotions or actions related to a partial or intermediate state. The "Ardha Pataka" mudra's grace and simplicity make it a versatile tool for Bharatanatyam dancers to express a wide range of sentiments and characters during their performances.


Kartari-Mukha:
Kartari-Mukha is a hand gesture where the thumb and the forefinger are brought together, creating a shape resembling a pair of scissors. This mudra symbolizes cutting or separating actions, making it a versatile gesture for depicting various scenarios involving division or detachment. Dancers use Kartari-Mukha to convey the idea of slicing through obstacles, severing connections, or emphasizing precision. It adds depth and nuance to the narrative, allowing the audience to interpret the dance's storyline with clarity and emotion. The Kartari-Mukha mudra's elegant and straightforward form complements the intricate expressions and movements of Bharatanatyam.

Mayura:
The "Mayura" hand gesture in Bharatanatyam is formed by bending the ring finger while extending the other fingers. This elegant mudra resembles the graceful head of a peacock. In the dance, the Mayura mudra is often used to depict the peacock, which holds great symbolism in Indian culture. The peacock is associated with beauty, grace, and vibrant colors, making it a symbol of aesthetic and divine qualities. Dancers employ the Mayura mudra to evoke these qualities and bring the essence of the peacock to life in their performances.



Ardha-Chandra:
Ardha-Chandra forms a graceful half-moon shape with the thumb and the little finger gently touching. It embodies the elegance and beauty of a crescent moon. In Bharatanatyam, Ardha Chandra is often associated with Lord Shiva and signifies aspects related to the moon's phases, symbolizing change and transformation. Dancers use this mudra to express the celestial, divine, or serene elements in their storytelling, evoking a sense of serenity and enchantment. Its subtlety and delicacy make Ardha Chandra a powerful tool for conveying emotions and moods in the dance.

Arala:
The "Araala" hand gesture in Bharatanatyam involves curling the fingers slightly with the thumb pressed against the base of the forefinger. This mudra is used to represent the beak of a bird, particularly a crow. The Araala mudra is often employed by dancers to depict birds in various dance narratives, symbolizing elements of nature or bird-like movements. Its distinct shape and placement of fingers allow for graceful and nuanced expressions when portraying avian characters or actions in a performance. Araala contributes to the richness of the dance vocabulary in Bharatanatyam by adding depth and authenticity to storytelling.

Shuka-Tundam:
Shuka-Tundam is a single-hand gesture in Bharatanatyam where the thumb and the middle finger are bent towards each other, resembling a parrot's beak. This mudra symbolizes a parrot or a bird's beak, often used to depict birds, playful actions, or interactions with nature in dance narratives. It adds a touch of grace and charm to the dancer's expressions, capturing the essence of the natural world and its vibrant inhabitants. Shuka-Tundam Hasta is one of the many Asamyukta Hastas (single-hand gestures) that enrich the storytelling aspect of Bharatanatyam, allowing dancers to convey intricate emotions and narratives with precision.

Mushti:
Mushti Hasta is a hand gesture in Bharatanatyam where the fingers are clenched into a tight fist, with the thumb covering the other fingers. This gesture symbolizes anger, rage, or a closed and aggressive emotion. It is often used in the dance to depict confrontational or intense moments in a narrative. Mushti Hasta is characterized by its forceful and firm appearance, contrasting with the open and graceful expressions of other hand gestures in the dance.


Shikharam:
The "Shikharam" gesture is one of the 28 Asamyukta Hastas in Bharatanatyam. It is a symbolic hand gesture that represents a temple tower. In this mudra, the thumb, forefinger, and little finger are extended, while the middle and ring fingers are bent. The extended fingers are joined at the tips to form a triangular shape, resembling the spire or pinnacle of a temple tower. The "Shikharam" mudra is often used in Bharatanatyam to depict architectural elements, religious symbolism, or references to temples in dance narratives. It adds a visual dimension to the storytelling, emphasizing the cultural and spiritual significance of temple architecture.

Kapitham:
Kapitham Mudra in Bharatanatyam is a hand gesture that represents a "Pigeon's Beak." In this mudra, the thumb is bent and touches the base of the forefinger, while the other fingers remain extended. The Kapitham Mudra is often used by dancers to depict a pigeon or dove, symbolizing grace and freedom in their performances. It is also employed to convey messages, communication, or gestures related to birds. This mudra's delicate and precise formation adds a touch of elegance and subtlety to the dancer's expressions, enhancing the storytelling aspect of Bharatanatyam.

Katakaa-Mukha:
The Katakamukha mudra represents a crab in Bharatanatyam. In this gesture, the thumb and the forefinger touch lightly while the other fingers remain curved. This mudra is often employed to depict aquatic themes, emphasizing the movements and characteristics of crabs. The graceful yet precise use of the Katakamukha mudra adds depth and authenticity to dance narratives related to underwater life, creating a vivid and captivating portrayal of aquatic creatures.


Suchi:
The Sucimukha mudra forms a shape resembling a needle. It is often used in Bharatanatyam to depict precise and intricate actions, such as sewing, embroidery, or any activity requiring attention to detail. The mudra symbolizes the focused and delicate nature of such actions, making it a powerful tool for the dancer to convey precision and skill in storytelling.



Chandrakala:
This Asamyukta Hasta forms a crescent moon shape by touching the thumb, forefinger, and little finger. It symbolizes the delicate and changing phases of the moon, reflecting the cyclical nature of life and time. In Bharatanatyam, it is often used to depict the moon's beauty, its connection to Lord Shiva, or to convey the passing of nights and the arrival of dawn. The Chandrakala mudra is significant in portraying various celestial and nocturnal themes within the dance, adding depth and symbolism to the storytelling. Its graceful form and evocative meaning make it a versatile element in Bharatanatyam performances.

Padmakosha:
The Padmakosha Hasta is a single-hand gesture in Bharatanatyam, where the thumb and the little finger are joined, forming a circular shape like a lotus bud. The other fingers remain extended. This mudra symbolizes the blooming of a lotus flower, representing purity, beauty, and divine grace. It is often used in the dance to depict the unfolding of a spiritual or metaphorical lotus, signifying the blossoming of the soul or the emergence of enlightenment. The Padmakosha Hasta adds a layer of elegance and symbolism to Bharatanatyam performances.

Sarpasheersha:
This hand gesture in Bharatanatyam resembles the shape of a serpent's head, with the fingers extended forward and slightly curved. It is commonly used in dance to represent serpent-related narratives and characters, often associated with Lord Shiva, who wears a serpent as a sacred ornament. The Sarpasheersha mudra can convey the presence of snakes in stories, symbolizing their power, mystique, and significance in Hindu mythology. It adds an element of mysticism and intrigue to the dance when portraying serpent-themed episodes. The graceful use of this mudra allows dancers to bring serpentine movements and expressions into their performances, captivating the audience with its symbolism and fluidity.

Mrugasheersha:
This hasta, or hand gesture, resembles a deer's head and is a graceful and elegant mudra used in Bharatanatyam. In Mrigasirsha Hasta, the thumb and the forefinger are brought together to form the shape of a deer's head, while the middle and ring fingers are bent gracefully. The little finger remains extended. This mudra is often employed by dancers to depict various aspects related to wildlife, forests, and the presence of deer in traditional narratives. Its delicate and refined posture adds a touch of serenity and beauty to the storytelling in the dance form.

Simhamukha:
This Asamyukta Hasta in Bharatanatyam forms a mudra resembling a lion's face. It is characterized by extending the thumb and little finger while bending the other fingers inward. Simhamukha symbolizes the majestic and powerful presence of a lion. Dancers use this mudra to portray characters or situations that exude strength, valor, or regal authority in their performances. The lion, often associated with royalty and bravery in Indian culture, finds representation through this gesture. Simhamukha adds a dramatic and dynamic element to the dance, enhancing the portrayal of characters and narratives.

Kangula:
The Kangula mudra in Bharatanatyam represents a bangle. It is formed by encircling the thumb and the forefinger to mimic the shape of a traditional Indian bangle. This mudra is often used to symbolize jewelry, adornment, or feminine grace in dance performances. Kangula adds a touch of elegance and beauty to the dancer's expressions, enhancing the overall aesthetic appeal. It is commonly employed in pieces that focus on themes of adornment, love, or celebration. The Kangula mudra, like other hand gestures in Bharatanatyam, contributes to the rich and intricate language of expression in this classical dance form.

Alapadma:
Alapadma is a hand gesture in Bharatanatyam that resembles a fully bloomed lotus. It is characterized by the thumb and little finger touching each other while the other three fingers are gracefully extended. This mudra symbolizes the exquisite beauty and purity associated with a fully open lotus flower. In dance, it is often used to convey themes of grace, elegance, and the blossoming of beauty. The Alapadma mudra is frequently employed to depict divine and celestial beings, adding a touch of serenity and aesthetic charm to the dancer's portrayal.

Chatura:
The Chatura hasta, in Bharatanatyam, is a mudra that signifies cleverness and intelligence. It is formed by lightly touching the thumb and forefinger. Dancers often employ this mudra to depict characters or situations that require sharp wit or astuteness. The Chatura mudra is a subtle yet powerful hand gesture that adds depth and nuance to the dance, allowing performers to convey the cleverness of their characters with elegance and grace. It is a versatile gesture used to communicate various aspects of intellect and cunning in classical dance storytelling.

Bhramara:
"Bhramara" is a specific hand gesture in Bharatanatyam, and it represents a bee. In this mudra, the fingers are held in a manner to resemble the shape of a bee. It is often used in dance to depict the delicate and graceful movements of a bee, adding a touch of nature and beauty to the performance. The Bhramara mudra is characterized by its precision and subtlety, allowing dancers to convey the imagery of bees buzzing around flowers with finesse. This mudra is a testament to the intricate vocabulary of hand gestures in Bharatanatyam, enabling dancers to tell stories with great detail and nuance.

Hamsasya:
The Hamsasya mudra is formed by gently touching the thumb and the forefinger. This mudra resembles a swan's beak and is often employed in Bharatanatyam to symbolize the grace and elegance of a swan. It is particularly useful for expressing delicate and lyrical emotions, and the dancer may use it to depict swan-like movements or portray characters associated with swans in classical narratives. The Hamsasya mudra adds a touch of refinement and subtlety to the dancer's expressions, evoking the imagery of this majestic bird in the dance's storytelling.

Hamsapaksha:
This mudra is formed by extending the thumb and the little finger, resembling a swan's wing. It symbolizes the graceful and expansive movements of a swan in flight. In Bharatanatyam, it is often used to convey the beauty, elegance, and serenity associated with swans. Dancers employ this mudra to depict avian imagery and to express emotions or actions related to the swan, such as floating or soaring gracefully. The Hamsapaksha mudra adds a touch of fluidity and majesty to the dancer's expressions, contributing to the overall aesthetic of the performance.

Samdamsha:
Samdamsha is one of the Asamyukta Hastas (single-hand gestures) in Bharatanatyam. In this mudra, the thumb is pressed against the tip of the forefinger, creating a gentle but firm connection. This mudra represents the act of holding or grasping something delicately, often signifying the delicate handling of objects or portraying characters engaged in activities such as picking flowers or holding a small item. The Samdamsha mudra adds a layer of nuance to the dancer's expressions, allowing them to convey intricate actions and emotions with subtlety and grace. It is an essential element in the repertoire of hand gestures that Bharatanatyam dancers use to tell captivating stories through their movements.

Mukula:
The Mukula hand gesture forms a closed, bud-like shape with the fingers and the thumb gently touching at the tips. It represents the budding or blooming of a flower. This gesture is often used to symbolize the beginning of a story, the emergence of a character, or the anticipation of something beautiful or auspicious. The Mukula mudra conveys a sense of potential and the promise of growth, making it a significant gesture in the expressive repertoire of Bharatanatyam.


Tamrachooda:
"Tamrachooda" is one of the names of Lord Krishna, particularly referring to His divine form when He wore a peacock feather (tamra means copper or peacock, and chooda means crown or crest) on His head. This name is often associated with His playful and charming aspect as the mischievous butter thief in Hindu mythology. It symbolizes His divine love and the enchantment He brings to His devotees through His melodious flute playing and divine leelas (divine actions). The image of Lord Krishna with the peacock feather in His hair is iconic and evokes feelings of devotion and admiration among His followers.

Trishoola:
Trishoola is a hand gesture in Bharatanatyam that forms the shape of a trident, a weapon associated with Lord Shiva. It is created by extending the thumb, forefinger, and ring finger while keeping the middle finger and little finger bent. The Trishoola mudra is symbolic of Lord Shiva's divine power and is often used to portray Shiva or convey aspects related to his presence and attributes in the dance. This gesture adds a touch of sacredness and strength to the performance, representing the deity's role as the destroyer of evil forces and the protector of the universe. In Bharatanatyam, every mudra carries deep cultural and spiritual significance, enriching the storytelling aspect of the dance.
